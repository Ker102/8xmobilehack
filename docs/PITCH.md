# Otto — Pitch

> A voice journal that turns your spoken day into an animated illustrated page — private by default, with one-tap sharing to a feed of the wildest pages people choose to make public.

**Decisions locked for this build:** Name **Otto** · Companion **Otto** (an octopus) · Wedge genre **Hackathons** · Brand: warm cream `#F6F3EA` + blue `#378ADD`, Geist Sans · Demo is a fully scripted, offline Expo web build.

---

## The one-liner

Speak your day. It becomes a beautiful animated page — narrated, illustrated, and yours. Share the wild ones to a feed built for doomscrolling.

## The insight

Two things just became true at once.

**Storytime is one of the internet's biggest content genres** — but it's stuck in an ugly medium: robotic text-to-speech over stolen gameplay footage. The stories are gold; the format is garbage.

**AI illustration just got fast and cheap enough** to render a personal story as a watchable animated page in seconds, for cents — not the slow, expensive, inconsistent mess that AI *video* still is.

Nobody has married a genuinely useful journaling habit to a genuinely shareable output. We do.

## What it is

You speak — your day, or a story worth telling. In under a minute it becomes an **animated illustrated page**: you, as a custom avatar, living out the moment, narrated in audio with captions baked on so it works with sound on *or* off.

Every page is private by default. The wild, funny, dramatic ones — the stuff you actually want to tell people — share to a feed in one tap. Otto, your octopus companion, is there through it all: it reacts to your day, remembers what matters, and quietly makes the next page more *you*.

- **Creation:** voice → animated illustrated page. This is the magic and the moat.
- **Journal:** a private, beautiful archive of your days. This is the habit.
- **Feed:** the pages people *choose* to make public, ranked for the scroll. This is the upside.
- **Companion:** Otto co-regulates and remembers — the relationship that makes journaling stick.

## Why Otto remembers

Memory is not there to make Otto feel like a chatbot with trivia about you. It exists to make journaling easier, more personal, and more continuous over time:

- **Better follow-up questions:** Otto can ask "How did the pitch go?" instead of "How was your day?"
- **Better pages:** the agent can reuse recurring people, places, projects, tone, symbols, and avatar details so entries feel like chapters in the same life, not isolated AI outputs.
- **Better reflection:** Otto can surface patterns across weeks — stress before demos, calm after walks, energy after building with friends — without making the product feel clinical.
- **Better recaps:** weekly/monthly summaries become personal because Otto knows what mattered, not just what was said most recently.
- **Better relationship:** visible, editable memory turns Otto from a formatting tool into a companion that meets you where you are.

The product principle: **Otto remembers just enough context to turn journaling from a blank page into an ongoing conversation.** Memory must stay visible, editable, and deletable so the user stays in control.

## Why this defends itself

**It's not a video — it's an animated page.** Still illustration with gentle motion: cheaper, faster, more consistent, and more visually distinctive than AI video. That's not a limitation — it's exactly what a high-volume feed needs to be economically viable and recognisable.

**It's not just a journal — it has a share layer and a feed.** The Strava model: natively a single-player tool, but the sharing layer is what turns a utility into a habit and a brand.

**It's not just another TikTok — it works for one user on day one.** This is the killer point. Feed-first social apps die at cold-start: an empty feed is worthless. A *journal* is useful to a single person immediately. So the feed is upside layered on an already-valuable tool — not life-support the whole company depends on.

## Why now

AI image generation crossed the cost/speed threshold this year that makes one rendered page per entry viable at scale. Two years ago this was impossible. In two years it's commoditised. The window is open now.

## The moat

The creation tool, not the algorithm. Anyone builds an engagement-ranked feed in a weekend. Almost nobody makes *"speak → beautiful animated page in under a minute"* work consistently and cheaply. That creation quality is a **supply advantage** — it fills a feed competitors structurally can't fill by cloning the UI. Magic creation → unique supply → a feed no one can copy.

## The honesty gradient (the thing we design around)

A journal's value is unguarded honesty. A feed tempts people to perform. We protect the core:

- Every entry is **private by default** — sacred, never auto-public.
- Sharing is a **deliberate, per-entry, one-tap act**.
- The shared artifact is the **pretty animated page**, not the raw confession — people share the aesthetic, not the vulnerability.

The mundane and the painful stay private; the wild and the funny flow to the feed. The "crazy stories feed" fills *organically* with the shareable subset — we don't force it.

## Go-to-market / the wedge: Hackathons

The journal half needs no seeding — it's useful alone. The *feed* half we seed ourselves with a few hundred showcase pages in **one saturated genre: hackathons**. Hackathons are dense, communal, story-rich, and recurring — every event is a fresh cohort of people living parallel all-nighters with shareable disasters and triumphs. We launch where we demo. One lane deep beats a scattered mix; expand genres only once the first is dense.

Distribution: every page is watermarked and one-tap exportable to TikTok / Reels — so the existing storytime audience becomes our top-of-funnel, and our feed is where creators stay.

## Trust & safety (we lead with this, we don't bury it)

A feed that rewards "craziest" points straight at fabrication, trauma-bait, and harm. From day one: **no real named people, nothing sexual, nothing involving minors, no how-to-harm content**, a report flow, and a clear line between wild-but-fine and harmful. Having a credible answer here is the difference between a fundable consumer-social company and an unfundable liability.

## Business model

Free to journal and scroll. Monetise creation: free tier with limits; subscription for unlimited pages, higher-quality renders, longer narration, and premium art styles. The habit is free; generation volume + quality is the paywall.

## The demo (hackathon framing)

We're **not** demoing a populated network — you can't build one in a day and shouldn't believe anyone who claims to. We demo the **magic**: speak (or type) a story, watch Otto turn it into an animated narrated page, live. The feed you'll scroll is pre-seeded with hackathon pages to show the experience. Otto the companion remembers a detail from your day and visibly folds it into your page.

What we're proving: (1) the creation is genuinely delightful, and (2) we have a specific, credible cold-start plan. That — the insight, the working magic, and a real answer to *"how do you fill the feed"* — is what's fundable.
